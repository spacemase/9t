Amanda Mae, Zen rock garden, on Washington Street in front of the Tashiro-Kaplan Building in Pioneer Square during this month's First Thursday, via JoMarie Jensen

To the temporary portable parks movement that started in San Francisco and is now practically everywhere (flickr photos), Amanda Mae in Seattle added a Zen wrinkle, tailored to geography, as Seattle faces Asia, not Europe.

Her friend, JoMarie Jensen, is also out and about, doing the regular Park(ing) on the meter thing with AstroTurf, lawn chairs and a potted tree, last seen on Sunday in front of the Seattle Art Museum.

The art team known as SuttonBeresCuller (represented by Lawrimore Project) not only has a park on wheels (2003), it also has a portable living room (2005), open to all comers.



Buster Simpson's landscapes were packed up and ready to go in cheap suitcases on the streets in Seattle in 2000. (Go to his Web site and click on Portable Landscapes.)

In the mid-'90s, the city of Anaheim in California asked him to develop some kind of site-specific art plan. Because Anaheim is basically an Etch-A-Sketch city, with developments that go up fast and come down faster, he recommended a series of big trees in pots on wheels to save the trouble of replanting. It's not on his Web site, so I guess it didn't happen.

Speaking of his Web site, it parties like it's 1999. An update would be nice. Artists who don't have galleries and haven't dumped images of their life's work on flickr (available in all sizes) make the press happy by maintaining good Web sites -- not that Buster is up nights, wondering what the art press, such as it is, thinks of him.
